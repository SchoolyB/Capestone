Gambia is a presidential and republican State with a democratic system of governance. In particular, three branches of the government share political powers. These include the executive, legislative, and judicial bodies held by elected or appointed officials. All these have been provided by the national Constitution drafted and ratified in 1996 and effective in 1997.

Executive power is vested on the President elected by direct votes of the electorates. The president by virtue of the presidential republic framework is deemed to be the head of the nation and the government. Administration of the presidential office is limited to five-year tenure. During the period, he is to appoint the ministers of the cabinet who are given delegated power limited to their respective functions.

Legislative power is granted to the National Assembly composed of 53 members. Among the legislators 48 are elected by popular vote while the five are appointed by the president. As of 2007, legislative slots composition according to political parties is characterized by Alliance for Patriotic Reorientation and Construction with 47 seats, United Democratic Party with two seats, National Alliance Democracy and Development with one seat, and one independent seat. All these legislative men occupy the post for five-year term.

Judicial power is lodged into an independent Supreme Court. The judiciary represented by this high court is mandated to work for the rule of justice and protection of human rights. National judicial framework is inspired by few influential legal systems such as English common law, Islamic law, and customary laws.
